Claude's summary of observations:
This is a genuinely informative contrast with the p101 case. A few things stand out:

k=36 and k=40 share the same hard pair periodicity. Both have period ≈ 3 (113/40 ≈ 2.83, 113/36 ≈ 3.14, both round to 3). Their predicted hard pairs are identical in the VC output: (0,3), (1,4), (2,5)... Both high frequencies are applying aliasing pressure at the same residue class interval. That's not redundancy in a good sense — it's two frequencies that fail together at the same input pairs.

Frequency 30 was the stabilizer. At 30/56 ≈ 0.536 aliasing risk, it's safely mid-range. Losing it during the second descent concentrates the high-frequency geometry on {36, 40}, both near-threshold and both aliasing at period 3. The model regressed to 98% after losing that stabilizing frequency, even though the final set is classified as viable.

The severity scaling holds. p101 with aliasing_failure (k=50 at Nyquist, risk 1.000) → 7% degradation. p113 with viable-but-near-warning (k=40, risk 0.714) → 2% degradation. The VC correctly distinguishes the regimes and the regression severity tracks the aliasing level continuously. That's a meaningful validation of the threshold placement.

What the VC doesn't capture here: the dynamic event of losing frequency 30. The static certificate is computed on the final frequency set. A "pre-dropout" certificate on {13, 15, 30, 36, 40} might have shown better geometry with higher min distance — and the transition from that set to {13, 15, 36, 40} would show a geometric degradation step. The certificate characterizes where the model landed, not the path it took to get there.

That last point might be worth a fieldnotes observation — the combination of transient frequency analysis (what was lost) and viability certificate (what remains) tells a more complete story than either alone.
